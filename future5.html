After a few years, climbing becomes more than just a few-times-a-week activity, it becomes a daily routine.
I see myself going to the gym every day and taking training seriously.
I go to my first outdoor climbing session with my friends, bringing crashpads and other equipment I've never used before.
I soon try top roping for the first time. Although I'm nervous about the height, I slowly become more confident.
Some of my friends start inviting me to small local competitions, and I figure I might as well try. At first, I'm nervous and fall more than I finish, but I try to keep my mindset the same as a normal session.
I start sending harder problems, and some of the holds that used to feel impossible now feel easy, showing my progress in outdoor climbing.
I begin competing in local comps more often, sending some routes with ease, but qualifying for the finals still feels out of reach.
After a few seasons of training and climbing outdoors, I finally get my first time slot in qualifiers for the finals.
I didn't win, nor I didn't even place in the top three, but I see how far I've come. The climbs that once felt impossible now feel natural to me. I remember why I started climbing in the first place: because it's fun, challenging, and full of problem-solving, just like when I first began years ago.
