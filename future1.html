I turn the corner of my apartment into a tiny studio and promise myself a year of composing.
Mornings begin with field recordings—train stations, rain gutters, whispered conversations.
Afternoons are for layering those textures with piano lines and soft percussion in my DAW.
A mentor at a local arts lab teaches me how to arrange for strings and modular synths.
My first EP, “Skyline Seeds,” slips onto streaming platforms and finds a few late-night listeners.
Indie filmmakers reach out, asking to license tracks for their quiet, thoughtful shorts.
I form a trio with a cellist and a spoken-word artist; we rehearse in an unused gallery space.
We host listening sessions with reactive projections so the room seems to breathe with the music.
For the first time I call myself a composer, and the word finally feels right.
A streaming platform hires me to score a mini-series about Pacific Northwest nightscapes.
Deadlines are fierce, so I build a rhythm that balances creativity with detailed session notes.
Collabs stretch across time zones—Tokyo at midnight, Berlin at dawn—trading stems through shared drives.
I launch a small label called Mosswave to release experimental albums from friends and mentors.
Our shows shift to rooftops with wireless headphones and live visuals that respond to the score.
To stay sharp I keep studying orchestration, build custom sample libraries, and learn new instruments.
Family and longtime friends keep me grounded, reminding me to take breaks between premieres.
A feature film with my soundtrack debuts at an international festival—a quiet, proud milestone.
Next dream: a residency where emerging artists can experiment with sound, light, and community together.
